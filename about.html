About Lauren

The person behind the message

She didn't set out to become a speaker. She set out to make sure no one was left out — and the story wrote itself from there.

She saw a gap. She filled it.

When Lauren Abela started at the University of Guelph-Humber, she noticed something missing: there was no place on campus for students with intellectual and developmental disabilities to be genuinely included — not just accommodated, but welcomed, valued, and seen.

So she and her co-founder built one. Together, they launched UofGH's first-ever Best Buddies chapter — a program that pairs students with and without IDD as friends, not charity cases. Lauren became Co-President of the chapter and poured herself into growing it into a real community.

The work paid off in ways she didn't expect. Best Buddies Canada recognized her with the inaugural Ambassador of Change Award. Then Best Buddies International selected Lauren and her buddy as Global Ambassadors for the Class of 2023 — the first Canadian buddy pair in the program's history to hold that distinction.

She flew to Indiana for the international Leadership Conference. She began monthly seminars with ambassadors from around the world. And she started researching how universities can make campus life genuinely inclusive for people with Down syndrome — not as a thesis topic, but because she actually wants it to change.

The message she carries

Lauren isn't a speaker because she decided it would be a good career. She's a speaker because people kept telling her that hearing her story changed something for them — and she realized that was worth showing up for.

"We want people to believe that they can do anything, they have a community — and to know that we have their backs."

Her message is simple at its core: belonging isn't something you find. It's something you build. And anyone can build it.

Today Lauren works as a Student Success Advisor at Humber Polytechnic, helping students navigate the academic and personal challenges of college life. She speaks at schools and conferences across Canada, sharing what she's lived — the gaps she saw, the things she built, and why she believes every room can be a place where people feel at home.
